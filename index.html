Berkshire Hathaway

From Failing Textile Mill to Apple’s Largest Shareholder

1. The Textile Origins (1880s–1965)

By the early 1960s, Berkshire Hathaway was a struggling textile company. Warren Buffett began buying shares in 1962, initially viewing it as a classic “cigar butt” investment — a cheap company with a few remaining profitable puffs.

2. Warren Buffett Takes Control (1965)

Buffett realized the textile business had no durable competitive advantage. Instead of reinvesting profits into more textile machinery, he redirected capital into insurance.

3. The Insurance Revolution (1967)

How Insurance Float Works

1️⃣ Customers pay premiums upfront.
2️⃣ Berkshire holds that money before claims are paid.
3️⃣ That money (the float) is invested in stocks, bonds, and businesses.
4️⃣ If underwriting is profitable, Berkshire gets paid to hold the float.

Float became the engine that powered Berkshire’s investment machine. Unlike debt, float often costs nothing — and sometimes generates profit.

4. Asset Allocation Mastery

Berkshire’s asset allocation philosophy rests on: - Permanent capital - Conservative leverage - Concentrated equity investments - Reinvestment of float

5. The Apple Investment (2016–Present)

The Apple investment demonstrated Buffett’s evolution: - Emphasis on ecosystem economics - Massive share repurchases increasing ownership percentage - Durable competitive advantage - Recurring revenue streams Apple eventually represented the largest single equity position in Berkshire’s portfolio — a remarkable transformation from a 19th‑century textile mill.

6. The Transformation in Perspective

1889: Berkshire Cotton Manufacturing founded.
1955: Berkshire and Hathaway merge.
1965: Buffett gains control.
1967: National Indemnity acquired.
1996: GEICO fully acquired.
2016: Apple investment begins.

From a failing New England textile manufacturer to one of the largest corporate shareholders in Apple, Berkshire Hathaway became a case study in capital allocation.
